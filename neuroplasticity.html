"Learning is a journey, not a destination".

Neuroplasticity from a Māori and Indigenous Perspective

Neuroplasticity refers to the brain's ability to rewire and adapt, strengthening new connections and discarding old ones. From a Māori perspective, this aligns with the concept of whakapapa (genealogy, interconnectedness), as learning is passed through generations and shaped by experiences, relationships, and wānanga (deep discussions or learning spaces).

Understanding neuroplasticity is beneficial because it reinforces te ao Māori (the Māori worldview) that learning and growth are lifelong journeys. It encourages individuals to embrace whakawhanaungatanga (relationships) and collective learning, recognising that healing, change, and personal transformation are possible through intentional actions. This is especially important for overcoming intergenerational trauma, reconnecting with cultural identity, and strengthening resilience.

Engaging with Neuroplasticity for Personal Growth

  • Te reo Māori me ōna tikanga (Māori language and customs): Actively engaging in learning te reo Māori rewires the brain, reinforcing identity and strengthening cognitive abilities.
  • Wairuatanga (spirituality): Meditation, karakia (prayers), and spending time in te taiao (nature) nurture the mind and enhance neuroplasticity through grounding and mindfulness.
  • Ako (reciprocal learning): Teaching what I learn strengthens my knowledge and supports others, reinforcing new neural pathways.

Ways to Increase Neuroplasticity

  • Kōrero and storytelling: Engaging in oral traditions strengthens memory and brain adaptability.
  • Movement and haka: Physical activities, including kapa haka, stimulate both body and mind, improving neural connections.
  • Whakaaro pai (positive thinking): Practicing gratitude and affirmations supports a growth mindset.
  • Exploring new skills: Learning traditional crafts like raranga (weaving) or mau rākau (Māori weaponry) challenges the brain and fosters new neural pathways.

Growth Mindset from a Māori and Indigenous Perspective

A growth mindset is the belief that abilities can develop through effort, learning, and persistence. In te ao Māori, this aligns with tū tangata (standing strong), emphasizing resilience, adaptability, and lifelong learning.

Understanding this concept made me reflect on whakamā (shame or self-doubt) and how it can prevent people from stepping into their full potential. Many Māori and Indigenous people experience whakamā due to colonial impacts, but a growth mindset shifts the focus from fixed limitations to continuous learning and self-empowerment.

Integrating Growth Mindset into My Learning Journey

  • Aroha ki te ako (love for learning): Approaching challenges with curiosity rather than fear.
  • Hapori ako (learning communities): Surrounding myself with supportive mentors and learners who encourage growth.
  • Kia mau ki te whāinga (staying focused on goals): Whether in tech, content creation, or fitness, I’ll remind myself that setbacks are learning opportunities, not failures.

Blending Neuroplasticity & Growth Mindset with My Learning Plan

Understanding these concepts influences my strategy for learning coding, content creation, and personal growth. I’ll:

  • Apply the ‘whakawhanaungatanga’ approach—learning in communities rather than in isolation.
  • Use ‘ako’ principles—teaching others strengthens my own understanding.
  • Balance ‘hinengaro, tinana, wairua’ (mind, body, and spirit)—integrating movement, mindfulness, and storytelling into my learning routines.
